1-1. Science of Science and field evolution

Science of Science (SciSci) is an interdisciplinary research area that uses large-scale data and computational methods to uncover the mechanisms underlying scientific activity [1][2]. The term itself was coined by Derek J. de Solla Price in 1963, when he proposed treating science as an object of systematic, quantitative study [3]. While related fields such as history of science, philosophy of science, sociology of science, scientometrics, and library and information science also study science, SciSci is distinguished by its computational approach to revealing the structures behind scientific practice.

The scope of SciSci extends well beyond traditional bibliometric indicators such as publication counts, citation networks, and co-authorship patterns. It encompasses the diverse interactions among agents involved in scientific activity, understood across geographic and temporal scales. For example, SciSci researchers analyze peer review systems to inform review policy [4], examine social biases embedded in science—including racial and gender disparities in editorial boards, review times, and citations [5][6]—and investigate how the status of research papers affects how they are read and cited [7]. SciSci also tackles more abstract and general phenomena, such as the quantification of individual scientific impact trajectories [8] and the tension between tradition and innovation in scientists’ research strategies [9].

A further distinctive feature of SciSci is its policy-oriented dimension. Beyond academic publications, SciSci leverages diverse research-related information to develop tools and policies that accelerate science. Studies on the commercialization of academic discoveries [10] and the public use and public funding of science [11] are representative of this strand. In recent years, the development of large-scale open datasets—including OpenAlex [12], Reliance on Science [13], and SciSciNet [14]—has significantly expanded what is possible, enabling new analyses and outputs across the field.

This project is one narrow contribution within this broader landscape. We do not model all scientific behavior. We focus on a specific task: describing the structural evolution of a research field (HCI) using topological methods, and evaluating whether such descriptions can complement existing frequency-based approaches.

1-2. Why HCI is especially difficult to summarize

HCI has multiple co-existing paradigms [15], diverse contribution types (systems, empirical studies, design, theory, methods), and contested core concepts. Hornbæk & Oulasvirta [16] argued that even the definition of “interaction” is not sufficiently shared.

This means simple frequency maps or single co-word networks cannot adequately capture how the field changes. In HCI, field change happens as re-arrangement of concepts, methods, and application areas—not just topic counts.

1-3. The research question

This project addresses three questions:

  1. RQ1: How do HCI subareas become adjacent before they stabilize as named topics?
  2. RQ2: Can early field reorganization be described in a way that is structural and interpretable?
  3. RQ3: Can such descriptions complement existing frequency-based maps of HCI evolution?
Transition. To answer these questions, we need a way of describing more than local similarity or direct co-occurrence. This is where topology becomes useful.
