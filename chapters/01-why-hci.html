Important caution.

Boundary description matters in HCI because the field is assembled from partially overlapping traditions that do not synchronize on the same timeline. Design methods, technical systems work, ethnographic traditions, and domain-specific application communities often move at different speeds, so meaningful reorganization appears first as patterned cross-neighborhood adjacency rather than immediate consensus around one topic label [1][2]. Without boundary-sensitive description, these early structural shifts are easy to miss: trend summaries may show only gradual vocabulary growth even when the internal architecture of concept relations is already changing.

This project therefore treats persistent loops as a way to describe intermediate structural states. A loop does not claim that a new field is already formed; instead, it marks a condition where concept regions are repeatedly proximate but not fully integrated. That distinction supports discussion with clearer foundations: we can ask whether a boundary is reorganizing, what terms mediate that reorganization, and which closure links remain absent, rather than jumping directly from local novelty to field-level conclusions [3][4].

Stable topics are not enough for this task because they are retrospective by construction. By the time a topic appears as a durable label cluster, much of the earlier boundary work has already occurred in mixed neighborhoods, transitional vocabularies, and uneven local closures. For HCI, where applied domains and methodological traditions continually recombine, tracking only stable topics can understate ongoing reorganization and can delay recognition of discussions that are structurally important but not yet lexically consolidated.
