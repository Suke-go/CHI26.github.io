2-1. What topology looks at

Topology studies connectivity patterns rather than coordinate details. Given a set of points, it reads clusters, loops, and filled regions from their arrangement.

2-2. Vietoris–Rips as growing connectivity

The Vietoris–Rips construction raises a distance threshold ε and connects all points within that distance. As ε increases, the structure changes step by step.

2-3. Persistent homology and H₁

Persistent homology tracks when structures appear (birth) and when they disappear (death). For H₁ features, we track loop-like holes. The persistence (death − birth) measures how long the hole survives across distance thresholds.

Mathematical detail (collapsible)

A feature is born at threshold εb and dies at εd. Persistence π = εd − εb. High persistence (π ≥ 0.05 in this project) signals a robust topological feature, not an artifact of a specific threshold choice.

The persistence diagram plots (birth, death) for each feature. Points far from the diagonal represent long-lived structures.

2-4. Birth edge, loop, and closure in this project

This research uses topology at three observation levels:

  • Local — birth edge: Which specific pair of concepts, when connected, creates the loop? This is the pair most structurally significant for that boundary.
  • Meso — persistent loop: The unfilled boundary structure that persists across thresholds, representing a structural void between communities.
  • Macro — yearly summaries: Aggregate measures (total persistence, triadic closure) that characterize the field's structural state each year.
Transition. With this intuition in place, we can now position our approach among existing ways of mapping research fields.
