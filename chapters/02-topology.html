This chapter explains the minimum topology background needed to read the rest of the site. A loop is a boundary-shaped relation among concepts, not a geometric object in physical space. The practical use is simple: loops show where multiple areas are connected but not yet fully integrated [1][2].

For readers new to persistent homology, treat the process as a controlled zoom. As the threshold grows, edges are added and shapes appear. Birth marks first visibility, persistence tracks how long a boundary remains open, and death marks closure by additional filling connections.

How to read this chapter together with later evidence chapters: use this as vocabulary, then inspect concrete representative cycles, then compare annual summaries. This avoids over-reading the method as if one exact object is tracked identically through every year [3].

Scope note. Persistent loops are descriptive structural signals. They do not by themselves establish causality, institutional strategy, or author intent.
Technical note (optional): compact formalization of H1 persistence

This formalization clarifies how the site defines and measures unresolved boundary structure.

Given concept points with pairwise distances, build a Vietoris–Rips complex at threshold ε by connecting points whose distance is at most ε; triangles appear when all three pairwise edges are present.

pi(gamma) = epsilon_d(gamma) - epsilon_b(gamma)
  • gamma: one H1 cycle candidate.
  • epsilon_b(gamma): threshold where gamma first appears (birth).
  • epsilon_d(gamma): threshold where gamma is filled (death).
  • pi(gamma): persistence length.

What this means here: H1 records loop-like features that are not filled by triangles. Larger persistence means the unresolved boundary pattern stays visible across a wider threshold range.
