Topological Bridges
Detecting Cross-Cluster Concept Relationships in HCI Research

What this site is about

Changes in HCI may occur before they become visible as named topics or frequent co-occurrences. This project asks how such early field reorganization can be described structurally and interpretably.

This supplementary site covers the background (Science of Science and HCI meta-science), a minimal introduction to topology, the relationship to existing bibliometric methods, and a guide to reading the analysis outputs.

The core question

Can we detect and describe the internal reorganization of HCI research fields before that reorganization stabilizes as a named topic or shows up in frequency-based maps?

Design philosophy. This site is not a topology tutorial. It shares how to read the internal reorganization of HCI as a field. Topology is introduced only to the minimum extent necessary for that purpose.

How to read this site

The site is organized into three layers:

  • Layer A — Grasp (Ch. 1): What problem is being addressed, and why it matters in HCI and SciSci.
  • Layer B — Understand (Ch. 2–5): Why topology is useful, how the analysis works, and how it relates to existing methods.
  • Layer C — Interpret (Ch. 6–9): What is found, how to read it, and where the limits are.

Visual intuition

At a glance

  • Data: ~30,000 papers from CHI, UIST, CSCW, UbiComp (1999–2024).
  • Method: Persistent homology (H₁) detects structural voids; representative cycles extract interpretable bridge candidates.
  • Key finding: H₁ bridges achieve 69.56% integration rate; median lead time of 2 years before integration.
  • Position: Not a prediction tool, but a structural diagnostic mirror for the community.

Evidence type: observational structural evidence with retrospective temporal validation. No causal claims.
